1. Purpose of Foundation‑1
Foundation‑1 defines the structure, behavior, and laws of a dimensional universe. It establishes the hierarchy of dimensions, the meaning of primitives, the role of manifolds, the function of substrates, and the rules that govern manifestation. Everything in the system must obey these laws to maintain consistency, determinism, and dimensional purity.

2. Dimensional Hierarchy
The universe unfolds through a sequence of emergent dimensions. Each dimension extends the previous one and introduces new capabilities.
• 	Point — identity, existence, singularity.
• 	Line — axes, directions, dimensionality.
• 	Plane — coordinates, adjacency, relationships.
• 	Volume — manifolds, continuous spaces of possibility.
• 	Object — entities, parameter bundles.
• 	Motion — change, interpolation, trajectories.
• 	Awareness — context, neighbors, deltas, visibility.
• 	Expression — substrates, manifestation packets.
• 	Source — universe identity, rules, and origin.
Rules governing this hierarchy:
• 	Dimensions must emerge in order.
• 	No dimension may reference a future dimension.
• 	Higher dimensions extend lower ones.
• 	Nothing exists outside Foundation‑1.

3. Primitives
Primitives are the smallest meaningful constructs. They define structure but do not store data or compute values.
Point
• 	Must have a unique identity.
• 	Cannot store attributes.
• 	Serves as the seed of all dimensions.
Dimension
• 	Defines an axis of variation (e.g., x, y, frequency).
• 	Cannot store values.
• 	Must be declared before use.
Wave Primitives
Shared by sound and light:
• 	Frequency
• 	Wavelength
• 	Amplitude
• 	Phase
• 	Bandwidth
Rules for primitives:
• 	Must be pure.
• 	Cannot compute.
• 	Define structure, not data.

4. Manifolds
A manifold is a continuous space of possibilities. It does not store values; it interprets coordinates.
Examples:
• 	Wave manifold
• 	Color manifold
• 	Sound manifold
• 	Spatial manifold
• 	Temporal manifold
Manifold rules:
• 	Must define their dimensions.
• 	Must be pure functions.
• 	Must not store state.
• 	Must not duplicate data.

5. Entities
Entities are parameter bundles that reference manifolds. They do not store attributes; they store coordinates used to sample manifolds.
Entity rules:
• 	Must use getters and setters.
• 	Cannot store redundant values.
• 	Must reference manifolds, not data.
Examples:
• 	SmartPixel
• 	SmartSound
• 	SmartObject

6. Processes
Processes are micro‑operations that perform minimal, context‑aware work. They activate only when needed and operate only on deltas.
Examples:
• 	Delta detection
• 	Neighbor propagation
• 	Visibility filtering
• 	Interpolation
• 	Absorption sampling
• 	Reflection sampling
Process rules:
• 	Must be stateless.
• 	Must be context‑aware.
• 	Must not compute global state.
• 	Must operate only on deltas.

7. Substrates
Substrates interpret manifolds and produce manifestation packets. They do not compute values; they resolve meaning from manifold coordinates.
Examples:
• 	Pixel substrate
• 	Sound channel substrate
• 	Lighting substrate
• 	Physics substrate
• 	Music substrate
Substrate rules:
• 	Must be pure.
• 	Cannot store state.
• 	Cannot compute values.
• 	Must interpret only.
• 	Must output manifestation packets.

8. Manifestation Packets
Manifestation packets are the only values that become bits and bytes. They represent the final resolved form of a manifold interpretation.
Examples:
• 	Pixel packet: x, y, color
• 	Sound packet: channel, frequency, amplitude
• 	Motion packet: position, velocity
• 	Light packet: hue, intensity
Packet rules:
• 	Packets are final.
• 	Cannot reference internal state.
• 	Must be deterministic.
• 	Must be minimal.

9. Context Rules
Context governs how entities interact with their environment.
• 	Only deltas are processed.
• 	Only visible/active entities are processed.
• 	Static geometry is never recomputed.
• 	Entities must know their neighbors.
• 	Entities must know their context.
• 	Manifolds are the single source of truth.

10. Non‑Redundancy Rules
To maintain purity and determinism:
• 	No value may be stored twice.
• 	No computation of known values.
• 	No caches of derived values.
• 	No global state.
• 	No hidden state.

11. Bits and Bytes
Bits and bytes are the final encoding layer for hardware. They do not contain meaning; they contain manifestation.
Rules:
• 	The engine never computes bits.
• 	The engine never stores bits.
• 	Hardware receives only packets.
• 	Bits represent manifestation, not meaning.

12. Identity and Continuity
Every entity, manifold, and substrate must have:
• 	A unique identity.
• 	A stable dimensional placement.
• 	Deterministic behavior.
• 	No cross‑dimension leaks.
